Introduction

You Did Everything Right

You've seen doctors, gotten scans, done PT, tried injections, maybe even surgery.

And you are still in pain.

This problem isn't that you aren't trying hard enough.

The problem is the explanation you were given, because it was VERY LIKELY wrong.

If it were right, you'd have fixed it by now with everything you've tried.

The American medical system spends $700 billion a year on chronic pain.

It prescribes more opioids than every other country on earth combined.

More procedures, more surgeries, more interventions than anywhere in the world. And chronic pain rates just keep climbing.

There's more people suffering, more people being treated, and more money being wasted.

That is exactly what happens when an entire system is treating the wrong thing.

I know this because I lived it through it extremely INTIMATELY.

My story started at 11 years old as a driven basketball player.

That with time the pain would go away.

Right around the patellar tendon.

So I went back to see this doctor and he told me that the pain was coming from patellar tendonitis, which he said most basketball players deal with.

He sent me to see a PT to do some treatment and said the pain should be gone in no time.

That was the start of 11 years of chronic debilitating pain in both of my knees which at its worst kept me from walking and going up stairs.

Over the next several 11 years, I saw 10+ doctors, 10+ PTs, and every other medical professional you can imagine.

Chiropractors, Acupuncture, I tried everything except surgery, there was always a part of me which was skeptical of medicine and had the idea that there should be a way to get my body pain-free without surgery.

I researched everything about the knees and knew the science behind patellar tendon strengthening like the back of my hand.

I literally tried every rehab program you will find.

One was so complex that I would drink orange juice with collagen powder before my 2 daily sessions of knee rehab while listening to a metronome.

That's what the science said was best, so I did it for years.

I kept looking for the newest treatment that would finally make the difference.

For 11 years I treated my body like something to be managed. I never felt truly free in it.

Every time I moved I was thinking about my knees and my fragile body.

Nothing worked. Month after month, year after year, same pain, different treatment, same result.

At 22, at the lowest point I can remember, I came across some information that I had never considered.

Not a new exercise or a better specialist. An explanation for my years of pain.

A framework based on the connection between the mind and the body. The premise was that the brain uses physical pain to protect us from emotions and thoughts that it perceives as dangerous.

I thought it was absolute BS!!!

I knew the anatomy of my knee better than most people in any room. I had the MRIs of my knees with the damage that doctors pointed to and said was the cause of my pain.

I read it anyway, only because I was EXTREMELY desperate.

And somewhere in those pages, something actually started resonating with me.

I went back to play college basketball as a graduate student, the sport I had quit years earlier, and I now live without a single worry of my knees and feel completely free in my body, like a kid.

What changed was not my body. What changed was my understanding of what was actually happening in it.

Chapter One

Two Models. One Is Wrong.

In the 1900s, the medical world made a paradigm shift. Dualism reigned supreme and the connection between mind and body was forgotten about.

Chronic pain has been explained the same way ever since.

Something in your body is damaged, misaligned, or weak.

A disc is herniated. A tendon is inflamed. Your hips are tight. Your glutes are not firing.

Your posture is off. One leg is slightly shorter than the other. Your core is not strong enough to support your spine.

Fix the structural problem, they'll say, and the pain goes away.

This is the traditional model.

And for millions of people in chronic pain, it has produced nothing but a longer list of things supposedly wrong with their body and a growing stack of treatments that never worked.

What the traditional model actually says

Under this framework, the body is essentially fragile.

It requires precise alignment, specific muscle activation patterns, and careful management to function without pain.

Deviate from that narrow window, sit wrong, lift wrong, develop a weakness somewhere, and the system breaks down and pain follows.

This is why you have been told to strengthen your glutes, stretch your hip flexors, fix your posture, avoid certain movements, get adjustments, and brace your core before lifting anything heavier than a bag of groceries.

The entire industry built around chronic pain, PT, chiropractic, sports medicine, corrective exercise, operates almost entirely within this model.

And there is an endless supply of new structural explanations waiting whenever the last one fails to hold up.

The problem is not that these practitioners are incompetent. The problem is that the model is wrong.

What the body is actually capable of

The human body is one of the most structurally resilient, adaptive systems ever created.

It was built to move, carry, absorb impact, recover from damage, and function under extreme conditions without falling apart.

For hundreds of thousands of years, humans walked barefoot on uneven terrain, carried heavy loads on their backs, squatted and bent and lifted without coaches, without corrective exercises, without anyone telling them which muscles to activate first.

And here is what the research actually shows.

The majority of people walking around today with no pain whatsoever have herniated discs, meniscus tears, labral tears, rotator cuff damage, and disc degeneration visible on imaging.

One large study found that over 50 percent of pain-free people in their forties had disc bulges on MRI.

Another found significant rotator cuff tears in people who had never experienced a single day of shoulder pain in their lives.

The findings were there. The pain was not.

Which means what showed up on your scan is VERY likely not the cause of your pain.

It is not damage in the way the word implies, something broken, something that needs fixing before you can be well.

It is normal tissue variation in a body that has been used. The same findings exist in millions of people who feel completely fine.

The scan found something to point at. That does not mean it found the answer.

The body does not need to be managed like a fragile machine.

It heals. It adapts. It compensates with insane intelligence.

The idea that a slightly tight hip, a minor disc irregularity, or a small tear in a tendon is responsible for years of debilitating chronic pain is not supported by the evidence. It never was.

What the traditional model calls damage is very often just a body that has lived in it. The pain has a different origin entirely.

The true model

Pain is not a structural alarm. It is a signal generated by the brain.

The brain receives constant input from every part of the body, processes that information alongside everything else it knows, your stress levels, your emotional state, your beliefs about your body, your past experiences, and makes a decision.

Is this person under threat? Does the body need protecting?

If the answer is yes, it produces pain. Not because something is necessarily damaged, but because the brain has decided protection is needed.

This means pain can exist with no structural cause.

It also means structural findings on an MRI, the disc bulge, the tear, the degeneration, can be completely irrelevant to the pain a person is experiencing.

Two patients with identical scans can have completely different pain levels. Two patients with completely different scans can have identical pain.

Because the scan is showing the body. The pain is coming from the brain.

Once you understand that, a lot of things that never made sense suddenly do.

Why your pain moves.

Why it is worse on Monday than Friday.

Why it flared up during the worst year of your life and settled down on holiday.

Why every structural treatment helped a little, temporarily, and then stopped.

Why you have been in pain for years when the body heals in months.

The structure was never the problem. Something else entirely is driving this.

Chapter Two

What Is Actually Causing Your Pain

You now know that pain is a signal generated by the brain, not a readout of damage in the body.

But that raises the obvious question.

If the pain is not coming from the structure, what is it coming from?

Pain has one job. Protection.

When you touch a hot stove, pain fires instantly to force you away from the threat.

When you break a bone, pain immobilizes the area so it can heal.

This is the brain doing exactly what it was designed to do, detecting perceived danger and generating a sensation powerful enough to change your behavior.

Nobody disputes this. Where it gets more interesting is when you understand that the brain does not only use pain to protect you from physical threats.

It uses pain to protect you from anything it perceives as dangerous.

The key word is perceived. The brain does not distinguish between what is actually dangerous and what it has been conditioned to believe is dangerous.

It simply scans for threat, physical, emotional, psychological, and responds.

And for many people, that threat is not a broken bone or torn tissue.

It is thoughts, emotions, memories, pressures, and beliefs that the brain has quietly filed under dangerous without you ever consciously deciding that.

Everyday emotions that feel manageable on the surface. Stress that seems normal. Pressure you have learned to push through. Situations you tell yourself you are fine with.

The brain does not need you to be in crisis for it to perceive danger. It needs only to assess, beneath the level of conscious awareness, that something requires protection.

And when it does, it generates pain.

Real, physical, measurable pain.

As a protection response to perceived threat.

This is what PDP is. Perceived Danger Pain.

The brain generating genuine physical symptoms in response to what it has assessed, consciously or not, as dangerous.

The triggers

The brain can learn to perceive danger in a remarkably wide range of things. Here are the most common triggers:

Emotions

Feelings the brain has decided are too intense or too dangerous to fully experience, frustration, unexpressed anger, grief, anxiety. Rather than letting those emotions surface, the brain creates a physical distraction.

Thoughts and thinking patterns

Catastrophic or worrisome thoughts about the future quietly keep the nervous system in a low-grade state of alarm. The brain reads that elevated state as a signal that something is wrong, and responds with pain.

Movement

Specific physical actions, bending, lifting, twisting, that were once painful can become permanently filed in the brain as threats. The brain fires pain the moment you attempt them, not because anything is being damaged, but because it learned to associate that movement with danger and has been reinforcing that lesson ever since.

Body positions

Sitting too long, standing, walking, positions that have become linked to past pain episodes. The brain sounds the alarm the moment you enter them, regardless of whether any tissue is actually under threat.

Self-judgment

Harsh internal criticism and self-evaluation act as a constant low-level stressor. The brain reads this as a threat to your wellbeing and responds accordingly.

Social stress

Difficult relationships, conflict, environments where you feel unsafe or unseen. The nervous system is highly attuned to social threat, it has been since we were tribal animals who depended on the group for survival.

Work stress

A demanding job, a difficult boss, a workload that never lets up. This often explains why pain is reliably worse during the week and mysteriously better on holiday, same body, different perceived threat level.

Financial stress

Economic pressure increases overall nervous system sensitivity and vigilance. A brain under constant financial strain is a brain that is always scanning for danger, and more likely to generate pain as a result.

The goal is not to eliminate all of these from your life.

That is neither possible nor the point. The point is that the brain has learned to perceive them as dangerous, and it does not have to.

That assessment can change. And when it does, the pain that was generated to protect you from them no longer has a reason to exist.

This is what PDP is. Perceived Danger Pain.

The brain generating genuine physical symptoms in response to what it has assessed, consciously or not, as dangerous.

Not a weakness. A protection mechanism that has been misfiring. And one that, once understood, can be retrained.

What belief does to your body

People who receive fake surgeries, real incisions, no procedure performed, report lasting relief that matches the outcomes of real operations.

Sugar pill patients show measurable changes in brain chemistry, immune function, and hormone levels.

Belief alone changes biology. This is one of the most replicated findings in all of medical research.

Now flip it.

When a doctor tells you your spine is degenerating, that bending is dangerous, that you need to be careful for the rest of your life, that is not neutral information.

That is a threat your nervous system is already responding to.

Studies show that patients who receive a more alarming diagnosis for the exact same condition report significantly more pain and have worse outcomes than those given a neutral explanation.

The diagnosis itself made them sicker. The doctor's words became the patient's biology.

Every MRI finding you were told to fear. Every movement you were warned to avoid. Every specialist who told you your body was fragile.

All of it landed in a brain that treats perceived danger as real danger, and responded accordingly.

The pain was real. The threat sustaining it was not.

And once you understand that, something that seemed impossible starts to become very clear.

If a false belief installed by a doctor can create and sustain real physical pain, then an accurate understanding of what is actually happening has the power to end it.

That is not wishful thinking.

That is exactly what the evidence shows, over and over again.

Chapter Three

The Person PDP Targets

PDP does not strike randomly.

There is a very specific type of person it tends to go after, and if you have been living with chronic pain that treatment after treatment has failed to fix, there is a good chance you are going to recognize yourself in what follows.

PDP tends to show up when the pressure a person places on themselves is high.

The years of building, proving, performing, and holding everything together.

This can be career demands, family responsibilities, financial pressure, athletic performance pressure, the relentless pursuit of a standard you set for yourself and cannot seem to walk away from.

The nervous system under sustained, self-generated demand, that is the environment PDP thrives in.

Perfectionism

The first trait is perfectionism.

High standards, a strong drive, and an inner bar that is always set just a little higher than where you currently are.

These traits are great for being successful, but holding yourself to relentlessly high expectations has a cost that rarely gets talked about.

The internal pressure never fully switches off.

And that sustained demand on the nervous system creates exactly the conditions PDP thrives in.

The pain is not a punishment.

It is the nervous system asking, for the first time, for a little room to breathe.

There is something interesting which my clients who are collegiate or professional athletes have mentioned to me many times which demonstrates this.

They have noticed that their teammates who don't do nearly as much rehab, treatment, or physical preparation don't have nearly the problems with their bodies as they do.

That is not a coincidence. The teammate who never thinks twice about their body has no fear or hyper-vigilance filed around movement, their nervous system is simply quiet.

But on top of that, the driven athlete is carrying significantly more internal pressure to perform, to justify their spot, to live up to the standards they have set for themselves.

That pressure runs constantly in the background, keeping the nervous system on alert.

PDP rarely targets the teammate who does not think about any of this.

It targets the one who cares the most.

Goodism

The second trait is goodism.

The need to be not just successful but good.

The person who says yes when they mean no and absorbs everyone else's problems without complaint.

The drive to achieve combined with the need to be liked creates a pressure that never gets a day off.

You are not just living your life, you are managing how it looks to everyone around you simultaneously.

And the resentment that builds from giving constantly while rarely receiving, from performing goodness as a strategy rather than a choice, has nowhere to go.

Expressing it would shatter the image. So it gets buried. And buried feelings have to go somewhere.

This is also why PDP rarely targets the selfish or the narcissistic.

Someone who freely expresses their frustration, who prioritizes their own needs without guilt, who never suppresses what they feel, that person is not building internal pressure.

There is nothing to contain. PDP requires someone who holds it all in. Someone too responsible, too driven, too self-critical to let themselves feel what they actually feel.

It targets the good ones. The reliable ones. The ones who never complain.

If that is you, you are in the right place.

The quote that says it all

The physician William Osler, often considered the father of modern medicine, said something that has never been more relevant than it is right now.

"It is more important to know what sort of person has the disease than what kind of disease the person has."

William Osler — Father of Modern Medicine

The pain is not in the MRI.

It is not in the disc or the tendon or the nerve.

It is in the person, their history, their patterns, the impossible standards they hold themselves to, and the needs they have spent a lifetime setting aside in the service of everything else.

When you understand the person, you understand the pain.

Chapter Four

8 Signs Your Pain Is PDP

This chapter is designed to give you something concrete.

A way to look at your own situation and assess whether what you have been experiencing fits the pattern of Perceived Danger Pain rather than a structural problem in the body.

Here are some signs:

1

Your pain appeared for no physical reason

No injury, no accident, no identifiable moment where something went wrong. You simply woke up one day and it was there. Structural damage has an origin. Pain that arrives out of nowhere does not.

2

Your pain is irregular

A structural injury follows a predictable pattern, it hurts when the damaged tissue is loaded and eases when it is not. PDP pain flares unpredictably, changes intensity without obvious reason, and behaves in ways that never quite map onto a consistent physical explanation.

3

Your pain moves or shifts location

Structural damage stays where it is. PDP pain moves, lower back to upper back, one knee to the other, shoulder to neck. When one area improves, another flares. The brain simply redirects to a new location.

4

Your pain is worse during stress and better on holiday

Same body, completely different pain levels depending on what is happening in your life. If the pain were structural, a week off would not change it. The fact that it does tells you something important about where it is coming from.

5

Your pain started during a major life transition

Look back at when it first appeared. A new job, a loss, a divorce, a period of sustained pressure. For many people there is a clear link between a major life event and the onset of chronic pain — one that never gets explored because the doctor is only looking at the body.

6

Treatments work temporarily but never fully resolve it

The physio helps for a while. The adjustment feels good for a day. The new program reduces things for a few weeks. But nothing ever lasts. This is the pattern of a nervous system whose underlying perceived threat has never been addressed.

7

You have been told your results are normal or inconsistent with your pain level

A doctor has looked at your scans and told you the findings do not fully explain what you are experiencing. That gap between what the imaging shows and what you feel is one of the clearest signals that the structural explanation is incomplete.

8

You have a history of moving symptoms

One chronic issue resolves and another appears somewhere else. Bad back for years, then the shoulder starts. The location changes but the pattern never fully goes away. That is not a fragile body. That is the same program running in a new location.

If you are reading this and nodding along, you are not imagining it. You are seeing your situation more clearly than most of the specialists you have visited ever did.

Which raises the obvious question, if this is what is actually happening, what does it take to resolve it?
